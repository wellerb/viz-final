Global CO2 Emissions and the Food Supply Chain

An Introduction to Greenhouse Gases

A greenhouse gas (GHG) is a gas that contributes to the “greenhouse effect” which traps heat within the Earth’s atmosphere causing the Earth's temperature to rise. GHG's have been increasing dramatically over the past century, and there are many reasons for this. Some of these causes are difficult to provide solutions for, like fossil fuels. Much of the world relies on fossil fuels for energy production-- so solving this problem is not so straight forward. However, there are some other factors which can be more easily mitigated, if we just change our own consumption methods. The factor we are speaking to specifically here is Food. The Food Supply Chain is one of the largest producers of GHG's (namely CO2); and, as the world populations continue to rise, the need for food has also risen dramatically. 1

In this data visualization story, we will be answering the question "How much of an impact does food production have on global greenhouse gas emissions?" with the hopes of providing information that can be used to allow our viewers to more readily understand not only their own role in the production of GHG's via their consumption of food, but to also hopefully provide possible solutions to help curb this growing problem.

CO2 Production: A Growing Problem


Carbon dioxide emissions are the main driver behind the climate change problem that we face in today's society. This global problem is widely known, and it is commonly recognized that these emissions need to be dramatically decreased, immediately.

We know that atmospheric CO2 concentration has been a growing problem, but how bad has it gotten?

This chart shows the concentration of CO2 in the atmosphere measured over the past two thousand years. As you can see, the atmospheric CO2 concentration remained relatively stagnant at 280 parts/million until the 1800s. Likely due to the technological and agricultrual boom of the 1800's, CO2 emissions began to climb.

And it has yet to slow down.

As you can see, the atmospheric CO2 concentration is increasing exponentially. And, in the future, it does not seem like it is going to slow down. As population continunes to increase, more energy and food will need to be produced, and thus, the CO2 concentration will continue to increase.2

CO2 Production: Global Trends by Country



From the above visualization, it is apparent that the CO2 concentration in the atmosphere is increasing exponentially. But, in order to draw conclusions about it, and to then philosophize how the issue could possibly be mitigated, we need a bit more information.



Who's producing all of it? Who's to blame?


As we saw in the first visualization, atmospheric CO2 concentration really started to increase in the 19th century. But, it actually began increasing at the greatest rate in the early 20th century. To the right of this description, is a scatterplot that extends from 1920 to the present day. As you can see, the CO2 production of the nations who produce the most CO2 generall was increasing-- some faster than others, but nearly all following a steady, positive linear slope. Specific conclusions that can be drawn speak to the nations that produce the most CO2-- China and the US. As the world's total CO2 production has more than tripled over the past 100 years, these two players were main producers of CO2.

We have seen that CO2 emissions have been on the rise and certain countries emit a lot of GHGs into the atmosphere. But what consumer sectors exactly are producing all of these harmful emissions? It's not surprising to see that transportation and electricity generation are the sectors with the highest percent of GHG emissions, but what about smaller, supposedly 'greener' sectors?

For this story, we chose to deep dive into the agriculture sector. Even though agriculture is generally considered a smaller producer, we as a group looked at our daily habits and decided that insight from this data might help us help ourselves and the environment. Hopefully by analyzing food, we would be able to see what specifics of the food industry are causing the biggest problems.

Now that we know what's going on, how serious it is that global levels of CO2 are rising, who the major players and contributors are, and we heve decided to look closely into the the agriculture sector, it's time to take a closer look at what exactly these contributions are that come from the Global Food Supply Chain.

Each Country's Most Produced Food Item






Even though the meat and dairy industry are known to release large amounts of greenhouse gases, we thought it would be interesting to analyze the countries who produce the most GHG's, specifically through their food supply chains. In the above vizualizations, we offer two different views of this data.

In the bubble chart, the size of the bubble is encoded on the amount of GHG's the country emits via their food supply chain. Thus, the larger bubbles are representative of countries that are reponsible for more GHG production from their food supply chain. On the click of a bubble, a new bar graph will appear that breaks down this GHG production further-- it tells us the products that each country produces that are the greatest producers (themeselves) of GHG's. The color of the bubble is indivative of which product was the highest GHG producing item in that country. For example, there is a lot of light green in the bubble chart-- this is because most countries highest GHG producing product is whole milk!

The other view we present is a map. The darker the shade of green the country is, the more GHG emissions from its food supply chain it is responsible for. As we can see China, the US, and India are the darkest shade of green which means they are responsible for producing food products that emit the more GHGs.

Share of Global CO2 Production and Percentage From Food Supply Chain

This grouped bar chart shows two different things. The dark green bar is representative of the percentage of global green house gas emissions each country is responsible for. The light green bar is the percentage of each country's green house gas production that comes from the food supply chain in that country. From this, we can infer a bit more of the causality of these emissions. It makes sense that China and Indonesia have a large fraction of their total GHG emissions coming from food production, because they have such large populations, that they must produce more food than other nations in order to keep everyone fed.

So now we understand that different countries produce different food products and those food products release a different amount of GHGs into our atmosphere. For example, chocolate was the highest global GHG producing food product, but it wasn't any countries single highest export. Whole milk was one of the most common products a country would produce.


CO2 Emissions and GDP: A Broadened View



This graph shows us CO2 Emissions when compared to a countries population and GDP. The bigger the bubble, the more populated that country is and the closer the bubble gets to the right, the higher its GDP. The United States is the top right larger yellow bubble while China is the largest green bubble with a smaller GDP. It is interesting to see how even though China looks like it does not produce a lot of CO2, we have to account for this visualization being per capita and China having a large population/large capita.

Now that we have a bit more context, and we see the impact each country commits to this global problem we can return to the original question at hand. How much of an impact does food production have on global greenhouse gas emissions? What was the point of all of this data?

Change in CO2: A positive outlook



This graph helps answer that question a little further. It shows us how the CO2 emissions have changed in the past few decades. As we can see, there has been a general trend for food production emissions to be increasing as the global GDP increases. Even though this is not ideal, we see that around the early 2010s, the GHG emission rate starts to level off. This is important because it means that countries started taking account of what GHGs they produce and started to make an effort to lessen the amount of emissions they released

We learned in the beginning the food produced GHGs only account for about 10% of the global GHG emissions, but we learned later on that some countries are more impacted by food produced GHGs than others. For example, China has a large percentage of GHGs coming from food emissions. We also learned that chocolate is the food product producing the highest levels of GHG emissions, while whole milk was deemed the most common food product per each country to produce the most GHG emissions. We found it interesting how whole milk and chocolate were the two biggest perpetrators of GHG emissions. It wasn't clear at first how they linked together but upon further analysis we determined that chocolate needs milk to be produced, so the chocolate data point is essentially combined with some aspects of milk causing it to be so high.

We learned to avoid not only meat as the media states, but also milk, chocolate and a variety of oils such as palm oil. It is encouraging to learn that food production emissions are lowering and continuing to lower as a nation's GDP increases and the country acknowledges the serverity of climate change and CO2 emissions, but it is our job to continue to be aware of what emissions are coming from what and to try to limit the amount of GHG that gets released into our atmosphere.
